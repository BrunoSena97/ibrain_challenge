Company Media Press Kit (CMPK)


Each startup is expected to deliver its own media press kit, which is a document containing information about your business, product and how it is going to be contextualized within the LGP Challenge.

Media press kits are mainly used at events and for launches to catch the eye of journalists and other media professionals, making them want to write an article, do an interview, etc. It should be a “one-stop shop” for all of the information media professionals need. A media press kit should contain your contact details, information about the company/products, a media release, images, videos, etc.

The media press kit should include the following items (see template and example attached):

First page
Startup logo (Can contain coloured background according to company id.)

Second Page
Product Name (Can be different from Startup name)
Product twitter-style pitch (max 140 characters)
Partner or customer Name (if applicable)
Project Description (max 700 characters)
Company Description (max 250 characters)
Team and roles/positions

Third Page
Project pictures/photographs. The grid can have changed modules.

In previous years, each Startup took a group photo, which can also be included in CMPK.

(Última edição: quarta-feira, 21 de fevereiro de 2024 às 17:16)
Resposta:

Company Name & Vision (CNV)

This deliverable is a crucial element of your company brand strategy; the brand strategy can help a business to get more customers and to have an easier time selling. Your company branding strategy starts with three essential statements:

  • Your company name: should reflect company culture and focus.
  • Your vision statement defines your long-term aspirations. It explains why you’re doing what you’re doing and the ultimate good you want to achieve through your success. Think of your vision as the picture of where you ultimately want your work to lead you.
  • Your mission statement defines the purpose of your work and the effect you intend to have on the world around you. It states what you do for others and the approach you follow as you aim to achieve the aspirations you’ve set for yourself, your organization, or your business. Think of your mission as the route you’ll follow to achieve your vision.

The value proposition will be later defined during the development of the business model.

(see branding for dummies)

(Última edição: quarta-feira, 28 de fevereiro de 2024 às 22:03)
Resposta:

Company & Teams Self-Assessment

Each startup company will carry a self-assessment on the performance of team members (including only members that were active during this phase of the project!) reporting to the entire project (must reflect all phases!). The outcome is a mark for each element of the startup company, respecting the following rules:

  1. Individual evaluation and awarding of marks must reflect the effort (40%) and the contribution for the results (60%) from each element. *
  2. The self-assessment must contain at least 3 different levels of performance (marks).
* Individual marks should always have supporting justifications and members not involved in the project should not be graded.

Marks are between 1 and 5, according to the performance of each member: 1 - extremely below average; 2 - below average; 3 - average; 4 - above average; 5 - well above average.

This assessment should be done by the company as a whole (the procedure should be discussed and agreed by all team members) and the final result record in the attached excel file.

KoM (what is the)

(Última edição: quinta-feira, 8 de fevereiro de 2024 às 12:29)
Resposta:

KoM (Kick-off Meeting)

Communication is critical to the success of any project and project team meetings are an excellent method of communication. The KoM (kick-off meeting) should take place even before the scope of the project is completely defined.

Goals

  • Mark the beginning of the project.
  • Build a shared vision of the project.
  • Establish a commitment among all involved in the project.
  • Motivate Team Members

Who should attend? It should bring together the entire project team, client and others who should officially recognize the beginning of the project.

Format

  • Approximate duration of 1 hour.
  • Initial part is based on a presentation (made by the client).
  • The final part of the meeting should allow discussion of some of the topics presented.
Topics
  • Presentation and contacts of the elements involved (team and client).
  • Objectives and scope of the project (client).
  • Schedule, milestones and main activities (team).
  • Benefits to customers and stakeholders (client).
  • Procedures for reporting problems and suggestions (team).
  • Main challenges (team).
  • Technology, multimedia and business needs (team).
  • Next steps (team).

After the meeting, the team must elaborate meeting minutes and share them with the client for validation (a procedure for validation should be discussed with the client); the meeting protocol must also be made available to the supervisor.

(Última edição: quinta-feira, 8 de fevereiro de 2024 às 12:28)
Resposta:

Meeting minutes

Minutes, also known as protocols or, informally, notes, are the instant written record of a meeting used to inform attendees and non-attendees about what was discussed or what happened during a meeting. They typically describe the events of the meeting and may include a list of attendees, a statement of the issues considered by the participants, and related responses or decisions for the issues.

Minutes from LGP meetings must include: date and location, list of participants, agenda items covered and decisions made by participants. It may be important to add action points also, with the corresponding responsible person and action deadline, if applicable.

Minimum Viable Product (what is the)

(Última edição: sexta-feira, 5 de abril de 2024 às 12:02)
Resposta:

Minimum Viable Product (MVP)

A minimum viable product (MVP) is a concept from Lean Startup that stresses the impact of learning in new product development. Eric Ries, defined an MVP as that version of a new product which allows a team to collect the maximum amount of validated learning about customers with the least effort.

In the context of LGP, the final result that will be handed to the client during the closure phase of the project is the MVP of the product vision developed during the ideation phase.

The team should prepare a document (or a set of documents) that includes:

  • requirements (use cases or user stories) developed in the project
  • architecture and technologies
  • information for the hand-over process (*)
  • administrator and user manuals (if applicable)
  • business model (**) for the product with focus on the strategic blocks (if relevant for the product)
  • findings and metrics measured from the build-measure-learn phase relevant for future developments

(*) The hand-over should be tailored to both the product and the client; could include handing over the code repository, setting up the product in the client production servers, etc. This process must be discussed and agreed with the client.

(**) The business model should be developed using based on the Business Model Canvas and mainly focusing on the value proposition, customer relationships, channels and customer segments.

Product Installation and Maintenance Packages (PIMP)

The PIMP is the main deliverable from the team to the client during the closure of the project. The contents of this deliverable must be discussed and agreed with the client and are part of the hand-over process.  The hand-over should be tailored to both the product and the client; could include handing over the code repository, setting up the product in the client production servers, etc.

Product Vision & Prototype (what is the)

(Última edição: quarta-feira, 28 de fevereiro de 2024 às 22:01)
Resposta:

Product Vision & Prototype (PVP)

This deliverable is a crucial element of your project and the cornerstone of your product strategy. The Product Vision imagines a product direction in the long-term. Basically, it represents the desired state for a product in the future, e.g. the ultimate choice for people who would like to live a gluten-free life, or an aim to significantly increase the mobility of people in wheel-chairs by creating a solution for them. 

A good Product Vision captures who the customer is, who the user is, and the value proposition and clearly links back to company objectives. Provides a common understanding of the direction you want to move towards and supports the Product Owner in prioritising what features to build first, in what order, and what not to build for the Product. 

Summing up, the Product Vision is a brief statement of the desired future state that would be achieved by developing and deploying a product.

A nice way to start with your Product Vision statement is by using the following template:

The Product Vision can be further developed and illustrated using the Product Vision Board. This board helps you to describe, visualise, and validate your product vision and strategy. It captures the target group, user needs, key product features, and business goals.

The (non-functional) Prototype is an early model or early release of your product that is built to test or demonstrate the concepts and approach. Ideally, it should be useful as a tool to communicate the Product Vision.

The Product Vision & Prototype deliverable should address the following subjects:
  • Product Vision statement & Product Vision Board
  • MVP (a subset of the Product Vision that will be developed within the project)
  • Prototype 

Additionally, this deliverable should also contain (minimal) documentation related to:

  • Requirements (use cases or user stories)
  • Architecture
  • Technologies (candidate or selected)

Project Charter (what is the)

(Última edição: quarta-feira, 21 de fevereiro de 2024 às 17:16)
Resposta:

Project Charter (PC)

What needs to be done when work is started? The process for creating a project charter is found at the intersection of the initiating process group and the integration knowledge area. In the beginning you need to prepare a high level description of project - the project charter - and identify stakeholders.

Inputs: business case and needs

Outputs: document with high-level information on the project/product or needs the projects intends to satisfy; should include: 

  • Introduction: explain the purpose of the project
  • Business Case, Project Statement, and Scope: unique project characteristics
  • Success Criteria: define the project success and list critical success factors
  • Major Requirements or Deliverables: describe the major stakeholder requirements or key project deliverables depending on how you choose to track your deliverables
  • Budget: cost estimate, information about spending authority (optional)
  • Schedule or Milestones: when will the project be complete, and what are the major milestones?
  • Constraints and Assumptions: what are the project’s known and unknown parameters at this point in time?
  • Summary of Risks: a high-level overview of major threats to the project’s success
  • Team and Organization: list who will work on the project, who oversees the project, and outline their roles
  • Approvals: a space for stakeholders to record their approval (or disapproval) of the charter document

Project Management Intermediate Report (what is the)

(Última edição: quinta-feira, 7 de março de 2024 às 22:37)
Resposta:

Project Management Intermediate Report

The  Project Management Intermediate Report summarises the main findings from the review phase retrospective. Should be provided to all project stakeholders (clients, supervisors and team members) to help to keep them up to date on the progress of the project and any pressing challenges the project may be facing.

The PMIR should be developed by the startup with active contribution from all members and reference complementary artefacts (e.g. project charter, weekly briefs, etc.). 

This retrospective should cover people, practices and plan. Results from the "Company & Teams Self-Assessment" and from the "Project Survival Test" provide input for the people and practices dimension. The plan dimension is based on the roadmap presented in the review meeting.The following topics should be addressed in the report:

  • Company overview (status and main difficulties faced)
  • Company description and organisation
  • Team coordination and work planning
  • Major risks and lessons learned (company-wise; creation of the company risk register) 
  • Project overview (status and main difficulties faced)
  • Project description and client
  • Project management practices (implemented and missing; inputs from the PST
  • Current scope compared to plan (Has the scope changed since the project began? If so, how?)
  • An overview of risks (Are there any high risks that need to be managed? creation of the risk register)
  • Project plan for the build-measure-learn phase (high-level plan but with a clear indication of planned releases; as presented in the review)

Some tips to write the project management report:

  • Be concise and share results and outcomes. Don’t focus on details your stakeholders don’t need to know. Try to use bullet points, not paragraphs. If you create a 100-page document, no one is likely to read it.
  • Provide context. Don’t just say a deliverable was late or low quality. How will this impact the rest of the project and what actions are being taken to resolve it? Stakeholders need to know how significant the problem is.
  • Make it visual. This allows stakeholders to quickly identify which projects are struggling and what areas they are struggling with, before reading further into the report.

Project Management Report (what is the)

(Última edição: sexta-feira, 5 de abril de 2024 às 12:03)
Resposta:

Project Management Report

The  Project Management Report is an overview of the final status of the project. It is a formal document of the state of a project at the closure phase. Should be provided to all project stakeholders (clients, supervisors and team members) to help keep them up to date on the project results and any valuable lessons learned during the project.

The PMR should be developed by the startup with active contribution from all members and reference complementary artefacts (e.g. project charter, weekly briefs, MVP, etc.). 

The following topics need to be addressed in the report:

  • Company description and organisation (and changes needed during the build-measure-learn phase)
  • Communication and coordination mechanisms
  • Team coordination and work planning
  • Major risks and lessons learned (analysis of the risk register; overview of risks faced (Were there any high risks that needed to be managed?) 
  • Project description and client
  • Project management practices and activities (highlighting changes implemented based on inputs from the PST)
  • Schedule progress against plan and degree of completion (Were all the project objectives achieved?)
  • Final scope compared to plan (Has the scope changed since the project began? If so, how?)
  • Planned versus actual resourcing (Are any resources missing or over-allocated? Weekly progress)
  • Current quality findings (Has quality testing been done? Were there any issues?)
  • Lessons learned (Strengths, weaknesses and what has been improved)
  • Plan for the hand-over

Some tips to write the project management report:

  • Be concise and share results and outcomes. Don’t focus on details your stakeholders don’t need to know. Try to use bullet points, not paragraphs. If you create a 100-page document, no one is likely to read it.
  • Provide context. Don’t just say a deliverable was late or low quality. How will this impact the rest of the project and what actions are being taken to resolve it? Stakeholders need to know how significant the problem is.
  • Make it visual. This allows stakeholders to quickly identify which projects are struggling and what areas they are struggling with, before reading further into the report.

Project Survival Test (what is the)

(Última edição: quarta-feira, 28 de fevereiro de 2024 às 22:02)
Resposta:

Project Survival Test

The Project Survival Test is an assessment instrument, which provides a “clue” to the project’s chances of successful completion: Will the software be delivered on time and within budget? Will it be a glorious achievement or an embarrassing mistake? To help you find out, the team should spend time analyzing and reflecting on the results of the survey.

If the test indicates the project is at risk, you can improve its condition by taking steps to raise its score. 

You should take the Project Survival Test at the start of the project, and then at each significant milestone. Project staffing and conditions can change, especially with large projects. As the project goes forward, more is learned about the technology, the people and the risks related to the project.

If the project is in the early stages, answer the questions based on the project plan. If the project is well underway, answer the questions based on what has actually happened on the project.

(based on the Steve McConnell, Software Project Survival Guide, Microsoft Press, 1997)

Pergunta:

Review (what is the)

(Última edição: terça-feira, 5 de março de 2024 às 10:53)
Resposta:

Review

The review meeting is a form of assessment in which "a team" of stakeholders (partners, supervisors, users, investors, ..) are involved to assess the company and its product. The review is the opportunity for companies to present the results achieved during the ideation phase and get feedback. Reviews involve multiple startup companies (3-4) and last 60-90 minutes. Each company has 20 minutes with 8-10 minutes for the company/product presentation and the remaining being used for Q&A.

Presentations are by default in Portuguese (unless companies have International members), having the teams the freedom to decide if slides (in case they are used) are in Portuguese or in English.

In the case of the products, the goal is to examine their suitability for their intended use and identify discrepancies between needs and desires. The product presentations should cover the following topics:

  1. Objective and motivation for the product/project (value for the client, competition, etc.)
  2. Functionalities (usage scenarios, prototypes, etc.)
  3. Architecture and technologies (high-level description of the architecture and technologies)
  4. Development plan (iterations, releases, priorities, etc)

Only the partners and supervisors, together with a couple of invited guests, will participate in the review. These participants in the review will be asked to assess the following topics of the presentation:

Startup Mission & Vision (10%)
Vision and product value (25%) (value proposition, differentiation, etc.)
MVP and Functionalities (20%) (usage scenarios, prototype, etc.)
Architecture & technologies (10%)
Development Roadmap (10%) (iterations and releases)
Overall presentation (10%)
Q&A (15%)

(Última edição: quinta-feira, 7 de março de 2024 às 22:37)
Resposta:

Risk Register (RR)

A project risk register is a tool project managers use to track and monitor any risks that might impact their projects. The purpose of the risk register is to identify, log, and track potential project risks.

A risk in project management is anything unexpected that could happen that would positively or negatively affect your project. Any time someone identifies something that could impact your project, it should be assessed by the team and recorded in the risk register. 

You need to keep in mind that the Risk Register is a living thing:

  • The information you put in it is changing rapidly.
  • Risks evolve and change attributes.
  • A Risk Response Plan may not provide the required efficiency.
  • Threats and opportunities may disappear, or they may become irrelevant.
Use the attached template to keep the company and projects risk register. This file should be kept in the company MS Teams channel.

(Última edição: quarta-feira, 28 de fevereiro de 2024 às 22:02)
Resposta:

Script for the Review (SFR)

The Script for the Review is a one-pager that contains the plan of the company for the review meeting; gives an overview of the approach (how is the company going to approach the review?) and then outlines the steps (what is going to be presented) and the responsibilities (who is presenting what).

Pergunta:

Weekly Brief (what is the)

(Última edição: quarta-feira, 21 de fevereiro de 2024 às 17:15)
Resposta:

Weekly Brief (WB)

The weekly brief is a summary of the work done during a week and how these activities contributed to the completion of a task or a project, or how each one brings the team closer to the achievement of their targets; does not have to contain too many details, just enough to make it informative to allow the supervisor to have an overall picture of how the company/team is performing and to focus the weekly meeting on the important topics.

The weekly briefs should be a one-pager prepared by the team leaders and include the following topics:

  • current status
  • plans for coming weeks
  • potential risks

The different sets of "happiness meters" complement the information provided in the weekly briefs.
